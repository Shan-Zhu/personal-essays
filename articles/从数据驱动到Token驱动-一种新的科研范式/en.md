# From Data-Driven to Token-Driven: A New Paradigm for Scientific Research

(1)

The paradigm of scientific research is, perhaps, undergoing a new transformation.

For the past two decades, "data-driven" has been the dominant theme in scientific methodology.

We built the Large Hadron Collider to generate data, launched space telescopes to collect data, and constructed gene sequencing pipelines to accumulate data. The core logic was clear: the world contains patterns, data is the carrier of patterns, and algorithms are the tools for extracting them.

But a new paradigm is quietly taking shape — the **Token-Driven Paradigm**.

Its core logic differs from the data-driven approach in subtle yet profound ways.

The generation of new knowledge no longer needs to begin with direct measurement of nature. Instead, it can emerge from **the recombination, compression, and reasoning over symbolic representations (tokens) of humanity's existing knowledge**.

This is not a rhetorical distinction.

AlphaFold predicts protein structures not by learning from new experimental data, but by learning from existing sequence-structure pairs — essentially tokenized biological knowledge.

DeepMind's GNoME predicted 2.2 million new stable materials from tokenized representations of existing crystal structures.

In drug discovery, the SMILES representation of molecules is a form of tokenization, and generative models "invent" new molecules in token space. In mathematics, LLM-assisted proofs are essentially searches for proof paths in token space, requiring no "data" at all.

These cases share a common thread: **new knowledge is generated within token space, not from new physical measurements.**

If the core operation of data-driven science is "extracting patterns from nature," then the core operation of token-driven science is **"reasoning from the totality of existing human knowledge to derive the parts that have not yet been written down."**

This shift may seem like a mere methodological iteration, but as we dig deeper, we find it touches on some of the most fundamental questions in science: What is the nature of knowledge? What does understanding mean? Where are the boundaries of science?

(2)

The greatest achievements of science have always been token operations.

Looking back at the history of science, many of the most profound theoretical breakthroughs were essentially operations in symbolic space.

Dirac predicted antimatter not from experimental data, but because his equation had a negative energy solution, and he refused to discard a mathematical symbol.

Maxwell added the displacement current term with no experimental basis whatsoever — purely because the symmetry of the equations was incomplete at the symbolic level.

Einstein constructed general relativity with thought experiments and the symbolic apparatus of Riemannian geometry as his core tools; experimental verification came afterward.

These were all gaps in purely symbolic structures.

What does this mean?

**The highest achievements of theoretical physics are essentially search and completion operations in mathematical symbol systems — or, one might say, in a kind of token space.**

So when we say the scientific paradigm is "shifting toward token-driven," that formulation needs correction. A more accurate statement would be:

Token-driven is not an entirely new paradigm — it is science finally recognizing the true nature of what it has been doing all along.

Large language models did not invent token operations as a research method. **They made the nature of this method visible.**

In the past, only a handful of geniuses — Dirac, Einstein, and their ilk — could perform efficient searches in symbolic space. Now, this capability is being industrialized.

(3)

If token-driven science merely made existing research methods more efficient, its significance would be limited.

But its impact goes far beyond that — it is reshaping the power structure of scientific discovery.

The data-driven paradigm has a hidden premise: **data is scarce, expensive, and often monopolized by a few institutions.**

You need CERN's collider, the Hubble telescope, multi-million-dollar gene sequencing equipment, or exclusive datasets accessible only in top laboratories. Data-driven science is inherently capital-intensive, naturally concentrating the power of scientific discovery in the hands of a few institutions.

The token-driven paradigm partially breaks this structure. When the raw material of knowledge shifts from "proprietary data" to "publicly available tokenized knowledge," a researcher in Nairobi and a researcher at MIT face, for the first time, nearly the same raw knowledge space.

But there is a darker corollary that should not be overlooked. If token-driven becomes the dominant paradigm, **compute becomes the new collider**.

The power structure of science will not truly democratize — it will merely shift from "those who own data" to "those who own compute."

From CERN to NVIDIA, from laboratories to data centers — the institutions change, but the concentration of power may remain the same, or even intensify.

Paradigm shifts are never purely scientific affairs — they are always political and economic as well.

(4)

Let us think even more deeply.

Traditional science carries an implicit promise: **we don't just want to predict — we want to understand.**

Newtonian mechanics doesn't just tell you that an apple will fall; it tells you why. "Understanding" — compressing complex phenomena into concise causal narratives — has always been considered the core value of science.

But the token-driven paradigm hints at a new possibility:

Perhaps "understanding" is merely a necessity born of limited human cognitive bandwidth — a compression strategy. When you have sufficient token-processing capacity, you don't need to "understand"; you just need to find the correct path in token space.

Humans need "understanding" because our working memory holds only 7±2 chunks, and we must compress complex phenomena into concise laws to operate on them.

A trillion-parameter model needs no such compression. It can navigate directly to the correct answer in high-dimensional token space, following a path that humans can never "understand."

AlphaFold predicts protein structures, but no biologist can fully explain its reasoning process. AlphaGo's Move 37 took human players months to barely understand why it was correct. Computer-assisted proofs have already appeared in mathematics — their correctness is verifiable, but no human can "read" all the reasoning.

If this trend continues, science may split into two activities:

**One is search in token space** — efficient, correct, but opaque.

**The other is the construction of human-comprehensible narratives** — inefficient, possibly flawed, but satisfying the human need for "meaning."

Then, might the second activity degenerate into post-hoc rationalization, much like we fabricate reasons for our intuitive decisions? If so, the very concept of "scientific understanding" may need to be redefined.

(5)

The power of the token-driven paradigm is immense, but it has a fundamental limit.

If we view "all existing scientific knowledge of humanity" as a formal system, then **there exist propositions that cannot be derived from within the current token system.**

**In other words, there are truths whose very concepts do not exist in our current token vocabulary.**

Suppose you were a super-AI in 1850, possessing tokenized representations of all human physics knowledge of that era. Could you derive quantum mechanics?

No. Because "wave function," "superposition," and "uncertainty principle" — these concepts did not exist in the token space of 1850. They are not combinations of existing concepts. When Planck introduced the quantum hypothesis, he was not searching within the existing token space — he was creating a new token.

Therefore, the token-driven paradigm faces at least three types of boundaries:

**The first is combinatorial gaps.** "We know A, we know B, but no one has ever considered A and B together" — for example, applying topology to condensed matter physics. All the necessary tokens already exist; they have simply never been correctly combined. Token-driven systems are extraordinarily powerful here, because they can naturally perceive gaps in cross-domain token co-occurrence structures.

**The second is representational gaps.** "There is a systematic blind spot in our existing conceptual framework, but we don't even know what the missing concept should look like" — much like how physics in 1850 lacked the concept of a "field." Token-driven systems can partially detect such gaps: the model's latent space may contain dimensions that correspond to no human-named concept. These "unnamed dimensions" are blurry projections of new concepts, but the system cannot clearly describe them using existing tokens.

**The third is framework gaps.** It is not that a particular concept is missing, but that the entire way we organize concepts has limitations. From Newtonian mechanics to quantum mechanics, what changed was not just a few concepts but the framework of "what a physical theory should look like" itself. Token-driven systems hit a hard boundary here, because their framework is predetermined by architecture and training processes. As Wittgenstein said: "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world."

(6)

The most profound significance of the token-driven paradigm lies not in how many new answers it can find within known space — though that alone is an enormous treasure. Its most profound significance lies in an unprecedented capability:

**Systematically, quantitatively, and at scale, mapping the boundaries of human cognition.**

How is such mapping possible? The key mechanism is **reading information from absence.**

A model trained on massive amounts of scientific literature tokens forms an enormous knowledge manifold internally. This manifold does not merely record "what is known" — it implicitly encodes "the structural relationships between known things." When unnatural voids, distortions, or inconsistencies appear in this structure, these topological anomalies are themselves signals.

Imagine this scenario: you ask a model to make predictions under certain specific conditions, and its output suddenly becomes highly uncertain, self-contradictory, or exhibits anomalous multimodality in its output distribution. This "confusion" is not the model's failure — it is marking fault lines in the knowledge structure.

This is an epistemological inversion: in traditional science, knowledge is signal and ignorance is noise. In token-driven boundary mapping, **ignorance itself becomes the signal.**

The most profound discoveries in the history of human science often began with noticing a structural absence.

Mendeleev saw not the known elements, but the gaps in the periodic table. Pauli saw the missing energy in beta decay. Astronomers saw the missing mass in galaxy rotation curves. But these discoveries were sporadic, accidental, dependent on the intuition of individual geniuses.

Token-driven systems make this "absence detection" systematic and engineerable for the first time.

(7)

If the above mechanism holds, the token-driven paradigm will produce an unprecedented meta-scientific object in human history:

A knowledge base of "what humanity currently does not know."

We have had encyclopedias of knowledge, from Diderot to Wikipedia, but we have never possessed an encyclopedia of ignorance.

The existence of this Book of Ignorance will have far-reaching consequences.

**First, it will change the logic of research resource allocation.** Currently, research funding is largely driven by sociological factors — which fields are trendy, which PIs are influential, which directions are easy to publish in. An ignorance map would provide an unprecedented objective reference: here is a massive cognitive void; the surrounding knowledge structure suggests something important and unknown may exist here, yet almost no one is studying it. For the first time, research funding agencies could allocate resources based on the topological features of the knowledge structure itself, rather than on peer review.

**Second, it will redefine "what counts as an important scientific question."** Currently, "important questions" are defined by disciplinary tradition, field elites, or prominent figures. The Book of Ignorance revealed by tokens cannot tell you what these questions are — because they lie beyond token space — but it can tell you where these questions project onto the known knowledge structure. Just as astronomers infer the distribution of dark matter through gravitational lensing: you cannot see dark matter, but you can see its distortion of visible light.

**Finally, and most profoundly, it may change science's understanding of its own mission.** The traditional self-narrative of science is that we are gradually revealing the truths of nature, and the unknown is steadily shrinking. But what if the ignorance map tells us that the more we know, the longer the "boundary of the unknown" we can survey becomes, not shorter?

The boundary of knowledge is a fractal structure: the higher the resolution, the longer the boundary line.

Science is not a container gradually being filled. It is an ever-expanding sphere — its volume is growing, but its surface area (the interface with the unknown) grows even faster. Token-driven systems allow us, for the first time, to quantitatively observe the rate of this surface area's growth.

This will force science to re-understand itself:

We have the opportunity to scientifically convert "not knowing what we don't know" into "knowing what we don't know."

(8)

The rise of the token-driven paradigm appears, on the surface, to be an upgrade in research tools — from training models with data to reasoning with tokenized knowledge. But as we dig deeper, we find that what it touches is far more profound than the tool level.

Yet at the same time, a fundamental limit exists. There are truths whose very concepts do not exist in our current token vocabulary — no operation within the token system can reach them.

And it is precisely this limit that points to the token-driven paradigm's most far-reaching contribution:

It will not cross the boundaries of cognition for us, but it will, for the first time, let us clearly see where those boundaries are and what shape they take.

On the other side of the boundary, something is faintly waiting for us.
